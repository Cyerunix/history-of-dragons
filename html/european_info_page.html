Dragons

Origin

The word "dragon" now serves as a sort of umbrella term for a large number of mythological serpentine creatures. The concept of the dragon emerged within many different cultures across the world, many of which had no prior contact with one another. This makes it very difficult to trace them back to a singular point. One of the earliest known depictions of a dragon comes from sculptures that were made sometime between 4500 and 3000 BC in China. The depiction here is much more similar to lung dragons, however, rather than the Western dragons we know today.

Another ancient depiction of the dragon that is slightly closer to Western dragons comes from Mesopotamia, circa 2100 BC. This draconic creature is known as the Mušḫuššu. They are similar to other mythological creatures such as the manticore or the chimera in that they are a sort of amalgamation of many different creatures. While it also differs significantly from the dragons we know and love today, it is also remarkably similar given how long ago it sprung up.

The first visual depiction of a dragon resembling the dragons we see in popular culture in more recent times can be traced back to circa 1260 AD. The dragon that was illustrated had a far longer tail than usual and an extra set of wings, but other than that, it looked to be quite similar to modern dragons.

Heraldry

Arguably one of the most notable case of dragons being used in heraldry is on the Welsh flag. The red dragon that is depicted on the flag comes from a story titled History of the Kings of Britain where a warlord named Vortigern tries to build a tower on a hill that happens to cover a pool housing two dragons. After the tower falls into the ground a few times, the warlord is informed of the dragon situation by a child prophet named Merlin. Upon discovering this, Vortigern drains the pool, exposing a red and a white dragon, both of which immediately begin to fight with each other. Merlin prophesizes that the white dragon, symbolizing England, will defeat the red dragon, symbolizing Wales, but the red dragon will return later to defeat the white dragon. This story represents the conquest of Wales by England, and the dragon on the Welsh flag later became symbolic of their eventual triumph.

Anatomy

Depictions of Western dragons (not to be confused with any other type of dragon) stemming all the way back to the Middle Ages have remained relatively consistent throughout history. Western dragons in particular are large, reptilian creatures with four legs, two wings, lots of horns and spikes, and a long tail.

Wyverns

Origin

The Wyvern's origin stems from the word vīpera in Latin. The implication behind the latin word comes from the wyvern's ability to use venom as its main weapon. It was considered to be a flying snake with two legs as it formed the shape of a dragon.

Heraldry

Oftentimes represented on English war banners during the early 8th century, Wyverns, overtime, became a symbol of courage and strength. Now, wyverns can be seen as a common logo or mascot, seeing lots of representation, especially in the country of Wales.

Anatomy

Wyverns are similar in body structure to dragons, but instead of having four legs and wings, their front legs are merged into their wings, leaving them with just four limbs instead of six.

Lindwurms

Origin

The lindwurm is a mythical creature in Northern and Central European folklore. They were known to live deep in the forest and traditionally have the shape of a giant serpent monster. They have origins in Sweden and much of Central Europe, all with their little differences of what one actually is.

Heraldry

A lindwurm in heraldry is basically a dragon without wings. Another definition for it would be a dragon with four feet and wings that are smaller than usual dragons. They would be discussed in tales and even depicted on military crests of certain villages and kingdoms.

Anatomy

In Swedish folklore, lindwurms appear as giant forest serpents without limbs, living between rocks deep in the forest. They were said to be dark in color with a brightly colored underside. Along its spine, it has fish-like dorsal fins and a mane similar to that of a horse. For defense and attack, it would spit out a milk-like substance that would blind its enemies. In Central Europe, the lindwurms usually resembled a dragon or something very similar to it. They usually appear with a scaly serpentine body, a dragon's head, and two clawed forelimbs. Some would also have wings. Most limbed depictions show that lindwurms do not walk on their limbs. Instead, they slither similarly to a snake and use their arms for traction.
